How Bonds Can Pay You Income Every Month,
No Matter Where the Market Goes Next

Let’s say you’re absolutely convinced that President Trump’s business-friendly policies will be great for the stock market. You might be tempted to dismiss bonds because their returns look paltry when compared to the S&P 500 during a bull market.

But we think that would be a big mistake for any investor who values stability and long-term success.

For one thing, there’s no guarantee the market will continue rising. It could even crash.

And like I mentioned above, in 2008 the overall bond index actually rose 5.2% while the S&P 500 fell 37%.

That means bonds outperformed stocks by a whopping 42 points in a single year during one ofthe worst downturns in modern history.

That’s why we believe it’s important to have at least some portion of your portfolio in rock-solid bonds, no matter what happens.

Not to mention that you’re probably underestimating bond investing’s impressive profit potential.

Some people dismiss bonds today because Treasury rates are so low. I’ve even seen breathless articles about a looming “bond apocalypse” on some financial news sites.

But bonds come in many forms. There are Treasury bonds, municipal bonds, investment-grade bonds, high-yield bonds, asset-backed bonds, corporate bonds, junk bonds, bond funds, bond ETFs, and more.

Some pay 1%, and some pay upwards of 15%. And some even offer 100% tax-free income!

Imagine a tax-free bond that pays 8% interest. When you factor in the tax benefits, that’s more like earning 10.8% interest (assuming a 35% tax rate). I challenge you to find yields like that anywhere else. You’re certainly not going to get them from CDs.

I understand why so many people disregard bonds. Heck, many of the younger analysts who work here at The Motley Fool avoid bonds altogether.

But we believe investors like you, looking to squeeze as much income as possible out of their portfolio, can’t afford to ignore them. Here are just a few of the benefits that bonds offer...

Municipal bonds are a powerful source of 100% tax-exempt income: Bond investors absolutely love municipal bonds because the interest income you earn is free from federal income taxes. And in most states, bonds issued by a government entity within the state are exempt from state and local income taxes as well.

Bonds offer a steady stream of predictable income for years on end: A bond issuer agrees to pay you back all of your money at the bond’s maturity date. That’s the total duration of the bond – it can be anywhere from one to 30 years – and you’ll receive checks from the issuer all along the way. Just imagine, 10 years from now you could still be collecting interest payments from an investment you make today.

A “bond ladder” ensures you receive a paycheck every single month: By investing in multiple bonds and staggering their payment dates, you can ensure you receive interest payments from your bond portfolio every month of the year.

To build a bond ladder, just break up your bond allocation into equal amounts and then buy individual bonds that mature at staggered times. So if you wanted to invest $100,000 in bonds,you’d put $20,000 each in bonds that mature in one, two, three, four, and five years.

It’s a simple way to earn guaranteed monthly income and protect yourself from rising interest rates (which can hurt a bond’s trading value).

5. How to use annuities in your income strategy... and how to tell which types are right for you

Do you want income that is guaranteed by an insurance company to last as long as you live, and is immune to the ups and downs of the stock and bond markets? Then consider an income annuity (also known as an immediate annuity or a fixed annuity). However, most annuities don't adjust for inflation, and the payouts are historically low due to low interest rates.

Do you want an investment that has the potential to grow and also has some built-in guarantees? You may want to consider a variable annuity with a so-called living benefit, such as a guaranteed level of income with the potential for higher income. This type of annuity is not cheap, and any of the growth would be taxed as ordinary income.

Do you want more tax-deferred growth and some protection from creditors? You might also want to look at a variable annuity. You won't pay taxes on the growth until you make withdrawals, at which point the money will be taxed as ordinary income.

Buying an annuity can easily cost you hundreds of thousands of dollars. If you’re even considering buying one, Robert Brokamp’s unbiased guidance on annuities alone could easily be worth the cost of admission to Motley Fool Total Income.
